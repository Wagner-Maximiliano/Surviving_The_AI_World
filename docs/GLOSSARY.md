# Glossary

## AGI

Artificial General Intelligence. A system that can learn and perform any intellectual task that a human can. No system achieves this today; estimates of when one might exist range from never to within two decades.

## AI

Artificial Intelligence. Any machine system trained to recognize patterns in data and make decisions based on those patterns. Modern AI systems excel at narrow tasks (recognizing faces, translating text) but do not think across domains the way humans do.

## Alignment

The technical problem of ensuring an AI system's goals match human values. As systems become more powerful, the cost of misalignment grows. A misaligned system with narrow goals (maximize profit, drive engagement) can cause real harm without intending it.

## Automation

The replacement of human labour with a machine process. Automation has been continuous for two centuries, from looms to spreadsheets. AI automation is faster in adoption and broader in reach — it displaces skill, not just physical effort.

## Disruption

Large-scale change that alters how an industry or society works. The term is often used as corporate jargon; here it means: a technology that makes existing skills and institutions suddenly worth less. Printing disrupted scribes. Electricity disrupted candlemakers. The timescale varies, but the outcome is real income loss for people in the disrupted field.

## Fine-tuning

Retraining an AI model on a specialized dataset to improve its performance on a specific task. Fine-tuning is cheaper than training from scratch, so a small organization can customize a large model to do its work better.

## Inference

Running a trained AI model to generate an output from a new input. The term separates the "learning phase" (training) from the "using phase" (inference). Inference is where the cost and speed of a model matter to the end user.

## LLM

Large Language Model. A neural network trained on vast text to predict what word comes next. GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini are LLMs. They can write, reason, and code, but they are not thinking in the way humans are — they are very good at pattern-matching at scale.

## Labour Market

The supply and demand for human work. Disruption hits the labour market when new machines reduce demand for a skill faster than workers can retrain. The impact lands in the households and communities where that skill concentrated, not evenly across the economy.

## Prompt Engineering

Writing instructions to an AI system to get better outputs. A well-written prompt can double the quality of an LLM's answer. As systems become more capable, the set of people who can write prompts well remains small — making prompt skill a valuable craft.
