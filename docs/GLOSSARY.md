# Glossary

Key terms used throughout the book.

## AGI

Artificial General Intelligence. A system that can perform intellectual tasks at least as well as a human across any domain, without domain-specific training. No AGI yet exists; the question of whether one will exist this century is open.

## AI

Artificial Intelligence. A software system trained on data to perform tasks that normally require human judgment. Includes narrow systems (chess engines, image classifiers) and hypothetical general systems.

## Alignment

The problem of building AI systems whose objectives match human intentions. A misaligned system optimizes for a formally specified goal that diverges from what humans actually want—classic example: a system tasked with "maximize human happiness" that wireheads the dopamine system.

## Automation

The use of machines or software to perform tasks previously done by humans, with reduced or no human intervention. In this book, used to mean cognitive automation—tasks of judgment and decision, not just routine physical work.

## Disruption

A sudden, significant change to an existing system or industry that displaces prior practices or workers. Used as a neutral descriptor of the change itself, not an evaluation.

## Fine-tuning

Training an existing AI model on new data to adapt it to a specific task or style. Starting from a pretrained model (like GPT-4) and continuing its training on a specialized dataset is faster and cheaper than training from scratch.

## Inference

Running a trained AI model on new input data to produce an output. The model does not learn during inference; it applies patterns learned during training. Inference on a large language model like GPT-4 takes seconds to minutes.

## Labour market

The supply and demand for human work. Changes here—job disappearance, skill premiums, wage pressure, geographic shifts—are often slower to appear than the underlying technology change but harder to reverse.

## LLM

Large Language Model. An AI system trained on vast amounts of text to predict the next word in a sequence. GPT-4, Claude, and Llama are LLMs. They do not "understand" in the human sense; they perform statistical pattern matching at scale.

## Prompt engineering

Crafting input text to an AI system (usually an LLM) to elicit a desired output. Techniques include specifying the format you want, providing examples, or asking the system to reason step-by-step before answering.
